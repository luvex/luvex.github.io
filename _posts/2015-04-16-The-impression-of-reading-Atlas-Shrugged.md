Recently, I finished reading a novel named *Atlas Shrugged* by Ayn Rand. The novel really impressed me, so I'd like to write down my feelings after reading it.
published: false

The book depicts a dystopian United States where many of society's most prominent and successful industrialists abandon their fortunes and the nation itself in response to aggressive new regulations, whereupon most vital industries collapse. The novel clearly advocates individualistic heroism and liberalism. The people are divided into two kinds: heroes who are so outstanding that they dominate the world, and people who the author sees as parasites — contributing little but always asking for a lot, and calling for strikes when unsatisfied. The author praises the heroes and looks down upon the others. Maybe the scene is a little extreme, but the author offers us a new way to think about our society: how to balance the interests of the masses and the elite. We have to admit that only a few people make great contributions to the course of human history. If we always emphasize that all men are created equal, is it truly fair?
published: false

There is also another interesting detail: the sentence *"Who is John Galt?"* appears frequently in the novel. It can be seen anywhere and said by any person. When I began reading, this recurring sentence confused me because John Galt didn't appear at all. But as I read on, the sentence seems to be a slogan — it represents the pursuit in the heroes' hearts.
published: false

In a word, the novel is so attractive because it makes me reflect on our society. Although some opinions in the novel are so rational that they could even be seen as cruelty, we should understand that compromise may be a good way to achieve peace, but will never be the way to promote social progress.
